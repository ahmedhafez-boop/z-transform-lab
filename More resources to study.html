From Laplace to Z-Transform¶

We know that we use the Laplace transform to work with continuous signals: $$ X(s) = \int_{0}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-st} dt = \int_{0}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-(\sigma + j\omega)t} dt = \int_{0}^{\infty} x(t) {\color{red}e^{-\sigma t}} {\color{blue}e^{-j\omega t}} dt $$ Laplace transform can give information about a continuous signal such as the region of stability, locations of poles and zeros, causality, etc., but it only works with continuous signals.

The DTFT (Discrete-Time Fourier Transform) solves this problem by allowing us to work with discrete signals: $$ X(e^{j\omega}) = \sum x[n]e^{-j\omega n} $$ It allows us to see the frequency response, but we can't identify the location of poles and zeros easily, as we only see the behavior on the imaginary axis (i.e., at $\sigma = 0$).

  • Frequency response
  • Spectrum

But it cannot give you:

  • Poles and zeros
  • Stability conditions
  • Regions of convergence (ROC)
  • Causal system behavior
  • Difference equation solutions
  • Filter design steps that require complex analysis
Transform For Type of signals Domain
Laplace Transform Continuous-time systems Continuous signals Complex plane (s-plane)
Z-Transform Discrete-time systems Digital signals (sampled) Complex plane (z-plane)
DTFT Frequency content only Discrete signals Unit circle (z = e^{jω})

We need a general approach to see the data for any $\sigma$, so we use the Z-transform: $$ X(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n] z^{-n} $$ where $z = r e^{j\omega}$.

Useful Summation Properties¶

$$ \sum_{n=a}^{b} \alpha = \alpha (b - a + 1) $$

$$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \alpha^n = \frac{1}{1 - \alpha}, \quad 0 < \alpha < 1 $$

$$ \sum_{n=0}^{b} \alpha^n = \frac{1 - \alpha^{b+1}}{1 - \alpha} $$

$$ \sum_{n=a}^{b} \alpha^n = \alpha^a \cdot \frac{1 - \alpha^{b-a+1}}{1 - \alpha} $$

Example 1: Exponential Sequence¶

$$ x[n] = a^n u[n] $$

Step 1: Start from definition

$$ X(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n z^{-n} $$

Step 2: Factor the expression

$$ X(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (a z^{-1})^n $$

Step 3: Recognize geometric series

This matches: $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \alpha^n = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} $$

Step 4: Apply formula

$$ X(z) = \frac{1}{1 - a z^{-1}}, \quad |z| > |a| $$

Example 2¶

$$ x[n] = u[-n-1] $$

Hence, the Z-transform is: $$ X(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{-1} z^{-n} $$

Let $k = -n$, so that $n = -k$. Then:

  • when $n = -1$, $k = 1$
  • when $n \to -\infty$, $k \to \infty$

Thus: $$ X(z) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} z^{k} $$

Using the identity: $$ \sum_{n=a}^{b} \alpha^n = \alpha^a \cdot \frac{1 - \alpha^{b-a+1}}{1 - \alpha} $$ Partial sum up to $b$: $$ \sum_{k=1}^{b} z^k = z^1 \cdot \frac{1 - z^{b-1+1}}{1 - z} = \frac{z(1 - z^b)}{1 - z} $$

Convergence¶

For convergence, we require $|z| < 1$, so that $z^b \to 0$ as $b \to \infty$. $$ X(z) = \frac{z}{1 - z}, \quad \text{ROC: } |z| < 1 $$

Symbolic Solver in MATLAB¶

This lab we will see how to use the symbolic solver to get the Z-transform for a function, we will use ztrans(F) and iztrans(F).

We already talked about syms and subs in the previous lab.

syms t a b

f = a*cos(b*t);

%% Method 1: Using subs
f_numeric1 = subs(f, [a, b], [3, pi]); % a=3, b=pi
t_vals = -pi:0.001:pi;
f_val1 = double(subs(f_numeric1, t, t_vals));

subplot(2,1,1)
plot(t_vals, f_val1)


%% Method 2: Using fplot with different parameters
f_numeric2 = subs(f, [a, b], [0.5, 2*pi]); % a=0.5, b=2*pi
subplot(2,1,2)

clc; clear all;
syms n z a b 
fx = a*cos(b*n);
fz = ztrans(fx, n, z);

fx_ = iztrans(fz, z, n);

% Substitute parameters and evaluate
t_vals = -1:0.01:1;

% Note: fx_ is a function of n (discrete), but we are evaluating it over continuous range here for visualization
fx_res = double(subs(fx_, [a b n], {2, pi, t_vals}));

plot(t_vals, fx_res)
title('Reconstructed Signal from Inverse Z-Transform')
xlabel('n')
ylabel('x[n]')
grid on
