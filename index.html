01 — INTUITION

Think of it as a zoom lens
for signals

You already know the Fourier Transform — it breaks a signal into its frequency ingredients. The Z-Transform does something more powerful: it works in the entire complex plane, not just on a circle.

Imagine frequency analysis as a map. The DTFT only lets you walk along the coastline (the unit circle). The Z-Transform gives you a helicopter — you can fly anywhere over the ocean.

02 — COMPARISON

DTFT vs. Z-Transform

Both transform a discrete-time signal into frequency domain — but they live in different spaces.

DTFT

Discrete-Time Fourier Transform

X(ejω) = Σ x[n] e−jωn
  • Variable ω is purely real
  • Only defined on the unit circle |z|=1
  • Doesn't converge for growing signals
  • Great for steady, stable signals
  • Think: a snapshot on the circle
Z-Transform

Z-Transform

X(z) = Σ x[n] z−n
  • Variable z is complex (z = rejω)
  • Defined over a region in the complex plane
  • Converges for many more signal types
  • Reveals poles & zeros — system behavior
  • Think: a full 3D landscape

06 — CHECK YOUR UNDERSTANDING

Quick Quiz

Where does the DTFT live in the Z-plane?
A. Anywhere in the complex plane
B. Only on the unit circle |z| = 1
C. Only on the real axis
D. Inside the unit disk |z| < 1
✅ Correct! The DTFT is essentially the Z-Transform evaluated on the unit circle — that's why it only gives you frequency information, not the full picture of system behavior.
A system is BIBO stable when all its poles are...
A. Outside the unit circle
B. Inside the unit circle |z| < 1
C. On the unit circle
D. At the origin
✅ Exactly! For a causal system to be stable, all poles must be strictly inside the unit circle. If a pole is outside, the impulse response grows forever — unstable!
What is the Z-transform of the unit impulse δ[n]?
A. z / (z−1)
B. 1
C. 1/z
D. z
✅ Perfect! δ[n] = 1 at n=0, zero elsewhere. So X(z) = 1·z⁰ = 1. Simple and elegant!

TASK — Z-TRANSFORM & INVERSE

Find X(z) and x[n] for the following signal:

x[n] = n · sin(15n) + e−3n

Hint — use MATLAB:

% ===== VERY SIMPLE Z-TRANSFORM DEMO =====
clear; close all; clc;

syms n z

% Define signal
x = n*sin(15*n) + exp(-3*n);

% Sample indices
N = 0:10;

% Original signal values
x_vals = double(subs(x, n, N));

% Z-transform
X = ztrans(x, n, z);

% Inverse Z-transform
x_rec = iztrans(X, z, n);
x_rec_vals = double(subs(x_rec, n, N));

% Display
disp('x[n] ='); pretty(x)
disp('X(z) ='); pretty(X)
disp('Recovered x[n] ='); pretty(x_rec)

% Plot
figure;

subplot(3,1,1)
stem(N, x_vals, 'filled')
title('Original Signal x[n]')
xlabel('n'); ylabel('Amplitude'); grid on;

subplot(3,1,2)
stem(N, x_rec_vals, 'filled')
title('Recovered Signal using iztrans')
xlabel('n'); ylabel('Amplitude'); grid on;

% ===== Pole-Zero Plot =====
[num, den] = numden(simplify(X));
z0 = double(solve(num, z));
p = double(solve(den, z));
disp('POLES:'); disp(p);
subplot(3,1,3)
theta = linspace(0, 2*pi, 200);
plot(cos(theta), sin(theta), '--'); hold on
plot(real(z0), imag(z0), 'o', real(p), imag(p), 'x')
title('Poles and Zeros')
grid on; axis equal;
